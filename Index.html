        <h1>Why I Started Likeminded Group Travel — And Why Travel Has Always Been My North Star</h1>

        <p class="article-subtitle">A note from Kelsey Fuller, Founder of Likeminded Group Travel</p>


```

    <p>There's a moment I keep coming back to.</p>

    <p>I'm standing on the deck of a liveaboard dive boat somewhere in the Coral Sea, the Great Barrier Reef stretching out beneath me in every direction. The water is impossibly blue. The world is completely quiet except for the sound of waves. And I remember thinking — <em>this is it. This is what life is supposed to feel like.</em></p>

    <p>I'd booked that trip alone. Flown to Australia alone. Showed up to that boat knowing nobody. And somewhere between the first dive and the last sunset, I felt more like myself than I had in years.</p>

    <p>That trip didn't just check a bucket list item. It cracked something open in me. And it's a big part of why Likeminded Group Travel exists today.</p>


    <h2>It Started Long Before Australia</h2>

    <p>My love of travel goes all the way back to a post-college decision that probably seemed a little crazy to the people around me at the time. Instead of settling into a traditional career path, I packed my bags and moved to Chile. I spent months working in hostels and bed and breakfasts across the country — immersing myself in local life, learning the rhythms of a place completely different from anything I'd known, and meeting people from every corner of the world.</p>

    <p>It was my first real solo travel experience. And it changed everything. I came home with a completely different understanding of the world — and of myself. Travel had shown me that the world was so much bigger and more beautiful than I'd realized — and that I was capable of navigating it on my own terms.</p>


    <h2>Ten Years in Hospitality — And What It Taught Me</h2>

    <p>After Chile, I built a career in hospitality. Hotels, specifically — nearly a decade of learning what it means to make people feel truly welcome. To anticipate what someone needs before they even know they need it. To create an environment where a stranger walks in and immediately feels at home.</p>

    <p>I loved that work. But if I'm honest, there was always something missing in the corporate hotel world. The scale of it meant that guests were often numbers on a spreadsheet rather than humans with stories. The personal touch — the part I loved most — got lost in the logistics.</p>

    <p>Then the company I was working for went bankrupt. And as destabilizing as that was, it gave me something unexpected: a moment of stillness. A forced pause in which I had to ask myself a question I'd been too busy to sit with.</p>


    <div class="pull-quote">What do I actually love most about this work?</div>


    <p>The answer came quickly and clearly. It was never the buildings. It was the people.</p>


    <h2>The Trip That Showed Me What Was Possible</h2>

    <p>Around the same time, I found myself at a significant crossroads in my personal life. I needed something. I just wasn't sure what. Then I saw it — an Instagram travel influencer I'd followed for years was hosting a group trip to Morocco. Something about it called to me in a way I couldn't quite explain. I didn't know anyone going. I just knew, in that quiet certain way you sometimes know things, that I needed to be on that trip.</p>

    <p>So I booked it.</p>

    <p>What happened in Morocco I can only describe as soul-nourishing. The colors, the chaos, the kindness of strangers, the magic of a place so completely unlike anything I'd ever experienced. But more than the destination — it was the people. A group of strangers who showed up individually and became, over the course of just a few days, something that felt remarkably like community.</p>

    <p>I came home with a renewed sense of self. With friendships I still cherish. And with absolute clarity about something I'd been circling for years: <em>this is what travel can do. And I want to help other people experience exactly this.</em></p>
```
